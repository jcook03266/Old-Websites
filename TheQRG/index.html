Our Story
Originating from the Innovation Labs at New York Institute of Technology, a group of 4 qc-interested engineering & comp sci students sat down to found NYIT's very first quantum computing club in 2019.



Desiring to expand their knowledge beyond the institution, The Quantum Research Group is a collective of students, professors, researchers, and industry experts across all disciplines in NYIT and New York City using an interdisciplinary lens to tackle the future's biggest and most exciting problems. This isn't just a computer science/engineering group, this is a group that needs everyone.

What is Quantum Computing?
Quantum computing is a deviation from the classical computational techniques we've become accustomed to, otherwise known as binary, or the 1's and 0's your computer reads whenever you type a letter on the screen. When you press a key on your keyboard,



an encoded electrical signal is transmitted from the keyboard to your computer's processor; the signal is then decoded into raw 1's and 0's to induce a command,



that command is then translated into the material you see on your monitor. Lengthy explanations aside, as you've witnessed previously, Quantum computers are exponentially more powerful than any regular computer you could possibly dream up, this is solely due to the fact that they follow Quantum physics, which essentially means that binary is rendered obsolete by an entirely different playing field's standards. Quantum computers use a little something called qubits.

Qubits are the bread and butter of quantum computing; being the sole reasoning behind the exponential performance leap generated by this technological pariah because of the physical principal with which they are associated with, known as superposition.



Superposition is otherwise known as the conjoined natural states of an object occuring at the same time. Say a cat was falling off a building in a fetal-esque position, but when it lands it goes prone, basically with superposition that cat assumes both of those positions in time at the same instance, thus defying what most normal humans have come to know as real world physics. Since a qubit can be both a one and a zero this results in more calculations being done in a smaller package, as well as less power consumption necessary to switch 1's to 0's and vice versa. All in all, qubits are the quintessential element of quantum physics; although we are still in the infantile stages of quantum computational techniques and application, the race to the future definitely looks promising, but it doesn't come without its hurdles.
